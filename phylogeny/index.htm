Systematics and Phylogenetics

    Reconstructing the evolutionary history of groups, i.e., the way that species came to be the way they are, requires an understanding of relationships among groups. The field of phylogenetics allows us to make hypotheses about relationships, using morphological or molecular data. In recent years, the practice of using DNA sequences, and now entire genomes, to reconstruct relationships has become increasingly common. The study of systematics is a broad and comprehensive discipline, containing such pursuits as phylogenetics, taxonomy (the classification of organisms into natural groups), and biogeography (the study of how species got to be where they are in the world). Under the broadest interpretation, systematics investigates everything that relates to the form, distribution, ecology, and evolutionary history of species, and systematics is limited only by the scope of the research questions that one might have.

Systematics of Menyanthaceae

    Menyanthaceae are a family of aquatic and wetland plants, and they are a major focus of the research done in the Tippery lab. Phylogenetic analyses using molecular (DNA) data have resolved the phylogeny depicted at right, which represents a sampling of nearly all species of genera except Nymphoides, which includes approximately 40-50 species. Major clades of taxonomic interest are indicated by letters. Support values for nodes are parsimony bootstrap (above) and Bayesian posterior probability (below). A corresponding tree with branch lengths (parsimony) is to the left. The tree also shows ancestral state reconstructions for reproductive system characters, which are discussed on the Reproductive System Evolution page: 'X' indicates the loss of heterostyly, the open and closed circles represent the origins of gynodioecy and dioecy, respectively.

    The tree at right was published in:
Tippery, N.P., Les, D.H., Padgett, D.J., and S.W.L. Jacobs. 2008. Generic circumscription in Menyanthaceae: a phylogenetic evaluation. Systematic Botany 33: 598-612. [ abstract ]
