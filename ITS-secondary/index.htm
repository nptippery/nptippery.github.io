ITS Secondary Structure

    The internal transcribed spacer (ITS) refers to two regions of DNA that are interspersed with the 18S, 5.8S, and 26S ribosomal RNA (rRNA) genes. The rRNA genes are strongly conserved for their function in protein synthesis (which depends on the secondary structure of their single-stranded RNA transcript). The spacer regions are much more variable and originally had no known function. Recent research, however, has determined that the ITS regions in fact function to cleave the rRNA genes from the RNA transcript of the whole ITS/rRNA region. The function of ITS, like the rRNA genes, depends on certain aspects of secondary structure, which are remarkably conserved across broad phylogenetic distances.
    Don Les and I have described a method to use secondary structure characteristics in a phylogenetic context to group taxa that have more similar ITS secondary structures. In an examination of Menyanthaceae taxa, we were able to recover a phylogenetic tree very similar to the tree that was obtained using nucleotide data in a more traditional phylogenetic analysis.

    The method and results detailed below are published in:
Tippery, N.P. and D.H. Les. 2008. Phylogenetic analysis of the internal transcribed spacer (ITS) region in Menyanthaceae using predicted secondary structure. Molecular Phylogenetics and Evolution 49: 526-537. [ abstract ]

ITS Subregions
    Seven stem subregions of ITS, four in ITS-1 and three in ITS-2, are strongly conserved within Menyanthaceae and correspond to similarly conserved regions in the related family Asteraceae. The illustration below depicts likely structures for the ITS-1 and ITS-2 regions of Nymphoides cordata, which were predictively modeled using an algorithm to minimize free energy. The structure of 5.8S, which is located between ITS-1 and ITS-2, is not shown.

Encoding Data
    In order to obtain data that can be used in phylogenetic analyses, the seven subregions were coded independently. The figure below depicts the data encoding process comparatively across three species. Nucleotides that form complementary base-pairs were coded with '1', and nucleotides without a complementary pair (either a mismatch or a gap) were given a '0'. The data were aligned among taxa to maximize structural and nucleotide sequence similarity. Gaps that resulted from alignment were encoded separately.

Phylogenetic Analyses

The right-hand tree in each figure shows the corresponding phylogram of one of the most-parsimonious trees. Ancestral State Reconstruction
    The ITS secondary structure data can be used additionally to infer ancestral states for the structure of ITS subregions, and thereby to investigate the mechanisms and constraints of evolution in the ITS-1 and ITS-2 RNA molecules. Data analyzed in this manner may help to elucidate functions of the ITS transcript that have not yet been identified.
