Morphology

    Organisms exhibit a vast variety of forms. The study of morphology (i.e., the diversity and structural composition of forms) can reveal wonderful insights into relationships among species and the evolution of diversity.

    Aquatic plants are an ideal system in which to study morphological evolution and function, because the ancestors of aquatic plants lived on land and were adapted to a terrestrial environment.

The Aquatic Environment

    Plants that have adapted to the aquatic environment certainly have water abundantly available to them, but there also are many challenges associated with living in water. Among these are decreased light transmission as they get farther from the water surface and lower availability of atmospheric gases (e.g., CO2 and O2). The diverse lineages of aquatic plants have employed a wide variety of morphological changes in order to utilize the resources of the aquatic environment.

    Some plants are rooted in the substrate and thus have access to soil nutrients, while others are unrooted. Some plants have part of their body at or above the water surface, thus making available atmospheric gases, whereas other plants live entirely underwater. Inflorescence Architecture

    Some of the most remarkable adaptations found in aquatic plants relate to their flowers. Only a few specialized aquatic plant groups allow their pollen to contact water, whereas the majority of aquatic plants retain flowers and reproductive modes reminiscent of their terrestrial ancestors.

    Species of Nymphoides (Menyanthaceae) have floating leaves and flowers that are pollinated above water. Flowering axes, termed inflorescences, differ among species by their relative flower positions and the arrangement of floating leaves that support their flowers. The diagram to the right shows the habit of a Nymphoides species having a cluster of flowers in close association with one floating leaf.

    We study the evolutionary history of the highly modified inflorescence morphology found in Nymphoides. The closest related plants to Nymphoides are emergent wetland plants that support often expansive inflorescences up to 100 cm (40 inches) tall! It becomes an interesting question, then, how such a reduced inflorescence could evolve.

    In an evolutionary context, we are considering the interaction between factors promoting the transition to a floating-leaved habit, and those that would be required to maintain dry flower pollination in an increasingly wet environment.

    Our tools for these questions include the reconstruction of evolutionary relationships through molecular phylogenetic methods, and the careful examination of developing inflorescences using dissection and microscopy. Results thus far have revealed a striking similarity among diverse Nymphoides species, and further work remains to be done comparing these to species in related genera.
